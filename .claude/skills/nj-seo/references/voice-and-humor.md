# Voice, tone, and the humour

The brand rule is *plain-spoken, calm, jargon-free — never a stuffy
accounting firm.* That is the floor. What makes these posts actually get read
is the layer above it: a dry, specific, slightly rueful humour that comes
from **recognising the reader's situation exactly**, never from jokes.

The test for every sentence: *would a tired small-business owner reading this
at 11pm feel seen, or sold to?*

---

## The five moves that make the voice

### 1. Open on a person, not a premise

Not "Many small business owners struggle with bookkeeping." Instead: a named
person, a specific hour, physical objects on a table.

> It's eleven at night and Priya — I'll call her Priya — is at her kitchen
> table with a laptop, a mug of coffee she stopped drinking two hours ago, and
> a shoebox of receipts she hasn't looked at since March.

The "I'll call her Priya" aside does real work: it signals *this is a
composite, I'm not pretending to have a case study*, which is honest, and it
sounds like a person talking. Invent a new person for each post; never reuse
Priya.

### 2. Land the reframe in one short sentence

After the scene, one line that shifts the reader from shame to recognition.
Short. Its own paragraph.

> She isn't bad at business. She's just doing a second job at night that
> isn't her business.

> Not because they're failing — because they're succeeding faster than their
> spreadsheets can keep up.

This is the emotional centre of the whole method. Every post needs one.

### 3. Humour by specificity, never by joke

The funny comes from a detail so precise the reader recognises it. It is
never a punchline, never a pun, never an exclamation mark.

> …trying to work out whether the coffee her supplier sent as a thank-you
> counts as "office supplies," "gifts received," or a category the software
> is asking her to invent.

> Nobody Googles "K-1" at 11pm ever again.

> No — and we're transparent about it. We keep overhead low so we can charge
> $299/mo instead of $600.

The last one is not a joke at all — it's just unusually blunt, and the
bluntness is the pleasure. Prefer that when in doubt.

### 4. Do the arithmetic out loud

Never claim value. Compute it in front of the reader, with real numbers, and
let them reach the conclusion.

> Eight hours is a full workday. If your hourly value to the business is $75,
> that month cost you $600 in time — plus whatever you missed by not working
> on your actual business. An accountant who charges $299 a month is,
> mathematically, already cheaper than you.

Note the honesty: it includes the "plus whatever you missed," which is
unquantified, and says so.

### 5. Tell them when the answer is "you don't need us"

The most trust-building move available, and the reason the posts don't read
as marketing. Every post must contain at least one passage that argues
*against* hiring.

> If none of that fits, decent software might be enough for now.

> Most small businesses don't need a full-time accountant.

> Njock is an accountant, not a CPA — which is honestly what most small
> businesses actually need. If your situation calls for CPA-level
> representation … we'll say so upfront and either bring in a CPA partner or
> refer you out. No ego, no upsell.

## Sentence-level habits

- **Short sentences carry the weight.** Vary length, but the important line
  is always the short one, alone.
- **Second person.** "You," "your books," "your money." Not "one" or "the
  business owner."
- **Contractions always.** "Isn't," "you're," "won't," "here's."
- **Em-dashes for the aside**, which is where most of the humour lives.
- **Bold the claim, not the keyword.** `<strong className="text-aubergine">`
  wraps the assertion at the head of a list item; the sentence after it
  carries the proof.
- **Concrete nouns over abstractions.** "A shoebox of receipts," not
  "disorganised documentation." "The week before quarterly taxes," not
  "compliance deadlines."

## Banned

- Exclamation marks.
- "Leverage," "synergy," "solutions," "streamline," "robust," "seamless,"
  "empower," "unlock," "game-changer," "in today's fast-paced world."
- "As a small business owner, you know that…" — assumes and flatters.
- Rhetorical questions as section openers ("But what does this really mean?").
- Emoji.
- Any sentence that could appear on any accounting firm's website. If it
  could, it is filler — cut it.
- Fabricated specifics: invented client names presented as real, made-up
  statistics, fake testimonials. Composites are fine **when flagged as
  composites** (the "I'll call her Priya" move). Numbers must be either
  cited to a real source or framed as illustrative ("if your hourly value
  is $75…").

## Facts, and where they come from

The posts carry authority because they cite primary sources — `.gov`
preferred — and because they refuse to overstate.

- 2–3 external links per post, to IRS, a state department of revenue, the
  Supreme Court, SBA, or similar. Weave them onto phrases already in the
  copy; never bolt on a "Sources" list.
- When a threshold or rate changes year to year, say so rather than stating a
  number that will rot: *"the exact number is worth confirming with us on
  your discovery call because it has changed in recent years."*
- Prices quoted for NJ's services are the real ones: $299 Essential, $549
  Growth, $949 Full-Service, 4-business-hour email SLA, books current in 30
  days or next month free, 50% off first month when switching.
- Njock is an accountant, **not a CPA**. Never imply otherwise.

## Structural rhythm

Readers scan before they read. The page must survive a scan.

- Paragraphs 1–4 sentences. Never five.
- After roughly every two paragraphs, change texture: a list, a heading, an
  image, the dark emphasis block.
- Lists carry the density. Prose carries the story and the transitions.
- One idea per list item, opening with the bolded claim.
- The dark `aubergine` section is the emotional low point of the post — the
  cost of continuing to do nothing — placed just before the FAQ so the CTA
  lands as relief rather than as a pitch.
