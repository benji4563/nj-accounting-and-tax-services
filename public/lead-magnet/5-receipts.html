For small-business owners

The 5 receipts most small businesses forget to save — and what they cost you.

Every year, small-business owners overpay their taxes because of receipts they didn't save. These five are the most commonly forgotten — and, based on the returns we've prepared, the most expensive.

1
Home-office utilities
If you run your business from a dedicated space at home, a portion of your electric, gas, internet, and even water bills is deductible. Save every monthly statement — even the PDF from your provider counts.
$600 – $1,800Typical annual deduction
2
Business mileage
Every drive to a client, supplier, the bank, or a work meeting counts — at 67¢ per mile in 2025. Most owners lose thousands here because they never track it. A free app like MileIQ takes 30 seconds to install.
$2,000 – $5,000Typical annual deduction
3
Client & prospect meals
Coffee with a lead. Lunch with a supplier. Dinner with a potential client. 50% deductible if the meal is directly business-related. Note the person's name and the topic on the back of the receipt — that's what the IRS wants to see.
$500 – $2,400Typical annual deduction
4
Software & subscriptions
QuickBooks, Adobe, Canva, LinkedIn Premium, industry magazines, your website hosting, your domain. Individually small — collectively big. If you use it to run your business, save the receipt.
$300 – $1,500Typical annual deduction
5
Continuing education
Courses, workshops, industry conferences, business books — anything that maintains or improves the skills you need to run your business. Even the flight and hotel to the conference are deductible.
$200 – $1,500Typical annual deduction
